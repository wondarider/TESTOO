A structured approach to protecting our loved ones from online harms

The internet is woven into our lives, especially for children and young people. While the online world brings a wealth of benefits, it can also increase the risk of harm and abuse. According to a UNICEF report, one in three people across 30 countries have reported being cyberbullied, this problem is only getting worse.

And while there are resources available to help teachers and parents become aware of the dangers and some tactics to help address them, there seems to be a gap that draws them together in a reachable manner that easily shows an entity how to establish a digital wellbeing program, measure its development and benchmark its performance.

To fill this gap, I am developing the Digital Wellbeing Security Framework - this both illustrates what ‘good protection’ looks like, and provides a structured approach to guide how to get there.

It is also a voluntary toolset that can assess, measure and produce a prioritised uplift roadmap for schools that already have some form of online safety program.

The DWSF is arranged like industry security frameworks, based on the CO:RE 4Cs classification of online risks and is inspired by existing online safety resources..
